Project Overview

In this project, you'll become a data detective at your school! You'll learn to collect information, organize it, and create colorful charts that tell interesting stories. By the end, you'll be able to spot patterns and share your discoveries with others.

Learning Objectives

  • Learn basic data collection techniques
  • Create simple visualizations
  • Identify patterns in everyday data
  • Present findings to peers

Skills You'll Gain

  • Data Collection
  • Basic Charts
  • Pattern Recognition
  • Presentation Skills

Expected Outcomes

  • Understanding of data collection methods
  • Basic visualization skills
  • Pattern recognition abilities
  • Presentation confidence
W1

Week 1: Choosing Your Topic & Planning

Decide what you want to investigate and create a plan

Activity 1.1: Brainstorming Topics

Think about things at your school that you're curious about. Here are some ideas to get you started:

Example Topics:
  • What are students' favorite subjects?
  • How do students get to school? (walk, bus, car, bike)
  • What types of lunch do students prefer?
  • How many books do students read per month?
  • What sports or activities are most popular?
  • What time do students usually wake up on school days?
  1. Write down 3-5 questions you'd like to answer about your school
  2. Choose ONE question that interests you most
  3. Make sure your question can be answered with simple data (numbers or categories)
  4. Share your chosen topic with your teacher for approval
Tip: Pick a topic that your classmates will find interesting too! This will make it easier to collect data.

Activity 1.2: Creating Your Survey

Now that you have your topic, it's time to create a survey to collect data from your classmates.

  1. Write your main question clearly at the top of your survey
  2. Create 3-5 answer choices (keep them simple and clear)
  3. Add space for respondents to write their name or mark anonymous
  4. Test your survey on 2-3 friends to make sure it's easy to understand
  5. Make any needed changes based on feedback
Example Survey:

Question: What is your favorite school subject?

Answer Choices:

  • ‚òê Mathematics
  • ‚òê Science
  • ‚òê English/Language Arts
  • ‚òê Social Studies
  • ‚òê Arts (Music, Drama, Visual Arts)
  • ‚òê Physical Education
  • ‚òê Other: ___________
Tip: You can create your survey on paper OR use Google Forms if you have computer access. Digital surveys are easier to collect and count!

Activity 1.3: Planning Your Data Collection

Create a plan for how and when you'll collect your data.

  1. Decide how many people you want to survey (aim for at least 30 responses)
  2. Plan where you'll collect data (classroom, playground, cafeteria)
  3. Ask your teacher for permission to survey during appropriate times
  4. Create a schedule: When will you collect data each day?
  5. Prepare materials: Make enough copies of your survey
Tip: The more people you survey, the more interesting patterns you might find! Try to get responses from different grades if possible.
W2

Week 2: Collecting & Organizing Data

Gather your survey responses and put them in order

Activity 2.1: Collecting Data

It's time to survey your classmates and collect responses!

  1. Introduce yourself and explain your project to each person
  2. Ask them to complete your survey honestly
  3. Thank each person for participating
  4. Keep your surveys organized in a folder or envelope
  5. Check off each response on your collection tracker
Tip: Be polite and respectful! If someone doesn't want to participate, that's okay. Move on to the next person.
What to Say:

"Hi! I'm doing a data science project about [your topic]. Would you be willing to answer one quick question for my survey? It will only take 10 seconds!"

Activity 2.2: Organizing Data in a Spreadsheet

Learn to use a spreadsheet (like Google Sheets or Excel) to organize your data.

  1. Open Google Sheets or Excel on a computer
  2. Create column headers: "Response Number", "Answer Choice", "Date Collected"
  3. Enter each survey response as a new row
  4. Use data validation to avoid typos (create a dropdown list of your answer choices)
  5. Double-check your entries for accuracy
Example Spreadsheet Layout:
Response # Favorite Subject Date
1 Mathematics 2025-01-15
2 Science 2025-01-15
3 Science 2025-01-16
Tip: Save your work frequently! Use File ‚Üí Save or press Ctrl+S (Cmd+S on Mac) every few minutes.

Activity 2.3: Counting and Summarizing

Learn to count how many times each answer was chosen and calculate percentages.

  1. Create a summary table with two columns: "Answer" and "Count"
  2. Use the COUNTIF function to count each answer automatically
  3. Add up all counts to get your total number of responses
  4. Calculate the percentage for each answer: (Count √∑ Total) √ó 100
  5. Round percentages to one decimal place
Example Summary Table:
Subject Count Percentage
Mathematics 8 26.7%
Science 12 40.0%
English 10 33.3%
TOTAL 30 100%
Formula Tip: In Google Sheets, use: =COUNTIF(B2:B31,"Science") to count how many times "Science" appears in column B.
W3

Week 3: Creating Charts & Presenting

Visualize your data with charts and share your findings

Activity 3.1: Creating Your First Chart

Learn to create colorful charts that show your data visually.

  1. Select your summary table data (Answer and Count columns)
  2. Click on "Insert" ‚Üí "Chart" in your spreadsheet
  3. Choose a chart type:
    • Bar Chart: Great for comparing categories
    • Pie Chart: Good for showing percentages of a whole
    • Column Chart: Similar to bar chart but vertical
  4. Add a title to your chart that explains what it shows
  5. Customize colors to make your chart attractive
  6. Add labels so people can understand the numbers
Tip: Try creating both a bar chart AND a pie chart with your data. Which one tells your story better?

Activity 3.2: Finding Patterns & Insights

Look at your data carefully and discover what it tells you.

  1. Study your chart and summary table carefully
  2. Write down 3-5 observations about what you see
  3. Identify the most popular and least popular answers
  4. Think about WHY the patterns exist
  5. Consider if the results surprise you or match what you expected
Example Observations:
  • "Science is the most popular subject with 40% of students choosing it."
  • "Mathematics and English are almost equally popular."
  • "More than half the class (73.3%) prefers either Science or English."
  • "Science might be popular because we recently did fun experiments."
Tip: Good data detectives ask "Why?" - Think about reasons behind the patterns you see!

Activity 3.3: Creating Your Presentation

Prepare to share your findings with your class.

  1. Create a simple presentation (Google Slides or PowerPoint) with 5-6 slides:
    • Slide 1: Title (Your question + Your name)
    • Slide 2: Why you chose this topic
    • Slide 3: How you collected data (number of people, methods)
    • Slide 4: Your chart(s) showing the results
    • Slide 5: Key findings and patterns
    • Slide 6: What you learned from this project
  2. Use large, easy-to-read fonts
  3. Add your colorful charts
  4. Keep text short and use bullet points
  5. Practice presenting to a friend or family member
Presentation Tips:
  • Speak clearly and look at your audience
  • Explain your charts - don't just read the slides
  • Be ready to answer questions
  • Practice timing - aim for 3-5 minutes

Activity 3.4: Presenting to Your Class

Share your data detective work with your classmates!

  1. Set up your presentation on the classroom computer/projector
  2. Take a deep breath and smile!
  3. Introduce yourself and your topic
  4. Go through each slide, explaining your findings
  5. Point out interesting patterns in your charts
  6. Ask if anyone has questions
  7. Thank your audience for listening
Tip: It's okay to be nervous! Remember, your classmates are interested in what you discovered. You're the expert on YOUR data!

Bonus Challenges (Optional)

Want to go further? Try these additional activities:

Compare Different Groups

Collect the same data from different grades and compare the results. Do younger and older students have different preferences?

Track Changes Over Time

Survey the same question at the beginning and end of the month. Has anything changed? Create a line graph to show the trend!

Create an Interactive Chart

Learn to use Chart.js or Google Charts to create an interactive chart on a website. Share it online with your class!

Collaborate with Other Data Detectives

Team up with classmates who chose different topics and create a "Data Detective Gallery" showcasing all your findings!
