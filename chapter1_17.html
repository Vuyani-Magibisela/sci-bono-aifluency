Introduction to Search Evolution

Before the Internet, when someone had a question, they could not simply turn to their mobile phone or computer for an answer. Instead, they had to search through physical books to find reliable information.

Before the internet, finding answers required tedious research through physical books and could take hours.

This task could take hours, depending on how close the local library was or whether the person had access to books at home. Nowadays, we can access information within seconds. This dramatic change in information accessibility is thanks to the evolution of search technologies.

The Early Days of Digital Search

The journey of search technology began in the 1970s, when Microsoft started with a vision to put "a computer on every desk and in every home." This was the start of personal computing, setting the stage for the future of search.

The evolution of Microsoft's search technologies from the 1970s to Bing's launch in 2009.

Two decades later, the Microsoft Network (MSN) emerged, signalling a new digital era. Then, in 1996, a significant breakthrough occurred with the launch of Windows NT 4.0, which introduced the Indexing Service. This innovation allowed for indexing of web content and text searches through Internet Information Services (IIS) servers.

The Indexing Service

The Indexing Service in Windows NT 4.0 was a pioneering technology that enabled the cataloging and searching of content on a computer or network. It created indexes of documents similar to how a book's index works, allowing for faster and more efficient searches of file contents rather than just filenames.

IIS servers are flexible, secure, and manageable web servers for hosting a variety of web content. The introduction of the Indexing Service marked a significant step towards a more efficient search experience, transforming how users could find information digitally.

The Rise of Modern Search Engines

In the early 2000s, MSN Search started using its own search algorithms, and by 2004 it began displaying its own search results. The pace of progress quickened, and in 2009, Microsoft unveiled Bing, a search engine designed to empower users to not only find but also comprehend information from the web.

Searching on Bing became intuitive: users simply type a query and the search engine quickly returns relevant results.

Searching on Bing is straightforward. You simply type a query and Bing searches the web for matching results, presenting them in an instant. For example, you could open Microsoft Edge, navigate to bing.com and ask: "How is the weather in New York City today?" Bing would promptly retrieve the answer.

The AI Revolution in Search

Today, Bing has evolved with new features that integrate AI capabilities. At the end of a weather report, for instance, you might notice options to ask follow-up questions like "How humid is it?" or "What is the chance of rain?" By selecting one of these questions, you transition to the Microsoft Copilot experience, previously known as Bing Chat.

From Search Engines to Reasoning Engines

During this transition, you are witnessing the reinvention of search. The new Copilot icon represents a shift from traditional search engines to AI-powered reasoning engines. While a search engine finds information, a reasoning engine understands and interprets information, paving the way for a more intelligent and interactive future of search.

The distinction between a search engine (finding information) and a reasoning engine (understanding and interpreting information).

From encyclopedias to a generative AI-powered experience like Copilot, the journey of search is a testament to human innovation and our quest for knowledge. This evolution marks a profound shift in how we access, process, and understand information in our daily lives.
